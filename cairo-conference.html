Cairo Conference, 1921

In March of 1921, officials from around the British Empire gathered to develop a coherent policy for the Middle East. The challenge at hand was daunting: balance French, British, Zionist, and local interests while redrawing borders and redistributing power. Although the League of Nations mandate system offered a temporary fix to the problem, anti-colonial sentiments and strategic consideration made the status quo unsustainable. Delegates will address these issues and more as they attempt to answer the most perplexing question in contemporary international politics: how can a sustainable solution in the Middle East be achieved? This committee will simulate actual conference proceedings; the full committee will frequently divide into subcommittees, forcing delegates to think on their feet and be ready to pivot at a moment's notice. Although crisis updates will drive debate and set the course of the weekend, group decisions (including both committee directives and joint crisis notes) will be the primary method for delegates to influence committee. Although the Cairo Conference featured a wide variety of clashing viewpoints, its participants shared the goal of creating a sustainable political climate in the Middle East. Keeping with this purpose, individual crisis notes and power plays will be less important than cooperation and consensus.
